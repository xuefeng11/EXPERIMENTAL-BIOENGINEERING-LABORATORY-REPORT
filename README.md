# Experimental Bioengineering Laboratory Report

**Bioanalytical Characterization of Small Molecules, Biomaterials, Proteins, and Nanoparticles Using UV–Vis, HPLC, ELISA, and DLS**

> A series of bioanalytical experiments characterizing small molecules, biomaterials, proteins, and nanoparticles across six analytical platforms — illustrating how analytical instrumentation can quantify molecular species, monitor biochemical reactions, and characterize nanomaterials relevant to biomedical and bioengineering applications.

**Author:** Xuefeng Liu
**Affiliation:** School of Medicine, Stanford University
**Email:** xfl@stanford.edu

## Overview

Modern bioengineering relies on analytical techniques that enable the characterization, quantification, and separation of chemical and biological species. This report documents experiments exploring several widely used methods, each providing complementary insight into molecular structure, concentration, reaction dynamics, and nanoparticle properties.

The objectives across the report were to (1) understand the physical principles underlying spectroscopic and chromatographic measurements, (2) develop practical skills operating modern analytical instruments, and (3) apply these techniques to determine concentrations, analyze mixtures, and characterize biological and nanoscale systems.

### Techniques covered

| Technique | Full Name | Primary Use |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| **UV–Vis** | Ultraviolet–Visible Spectroscopy | Absorbance-based identification and quantification (Beer–Lambert law) |
| **HPLC** | High-Performance Liquid Chromatography | Separation, identification, and quantification of mixtures |
| **Encapsulation** | Lipid micelles & hydrogels | Dye encapsulation for drug-delivery model systems |
| **Enzyme Kinetics** | UV–Vis reaction monitoring | Michaelis–Menten analysis of catalytic hydrolysis |
| **ELISA** | Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay | Antibody-based protein detection |
| **DLS** | Dynamic Light Scattering | Nanoparticle size and polydispersity characterization |

## Contents

Each chapter follows a consistent structure: *Abstract → Introduction → Method → Materials → Results → Discussion → Conclusion*.

### Chapter 2 — UV-Visible Spectroscopy to Determine Optical Behavior of Several Species
Uses light–matter interactions to (1) determine analyte concentration via the Beer–Lambert law and (2) relate chemical structure to light absorption. Molecules with conjugated π-systems absorb strongly, while simple sugars show minimal absorbance.

Analytes: Caffeine · Acetaminophen · Aspartame · Saccharin · Sucralose · D-(+)-Glucose · D-(−)-Lactose · Gold nanoparticles

### Chapter 3 — High Performance Liquid Chromatography in Separation and Identification of Mixed Compounds
Reverse-phase separation on an **Agilent 1260 Infinity II** system with a diode array detector (DAD), refractive index detector (RID), and a **4.6 × 100 mm C18 column**. Flow rate, injection volume, and mobile-phase ratio were varied to analyze caffeine, acetaminophen, and aspartame standards, then identify and quantify components of an unknown mixture using characteristic retention times and multi-wavelength confirmation.

### Chapter 4 — Encapsulation of Dyes in Lipid Micelles & Hydrogels
- **4.1 — Nile Red in lipid micelles:** Encapsulation efficiency of the hydrophobic dye Nile Red in micelles formed by SDS, CTAB, and Triton X-100.
- **4.2 — Dyes in extended-release hydrogels:** Encapsulation and release behavior of dyes in hydrogel matrices.

### Chapter 5 — Enzyme Kinetics
UV–Vis monitoring (~405 nm, 4-nitrophenolate product) of two hydrolysis systems comparing biological vs. chemical catalysis:
- Enzymatic hydrolysis of **4-nitrophenyl phosphate** by Shrimp Alkaline Phosphatase (SAP)
- Imidazole-catalyzed hydrolysis of **4-nitrophenyl acetate**

Reaction rates from initial slopes were fit to the **Michaelis–Menten** model to extract *V*max and *K*m.

### Chapter 6 — ELISA Analysis of Milk Whey Proteins
Indirect ELISA (EDVOTEK Kit #266) to quantify whey proteins across dairy and dairy-substitute samples, with relevance to nutritional analysis and food-allergen detection.


### Chapter 7 — Dynamic Light Scattering (DLS) Analysis of Nanoparticles
Characterization of gold nanoparticles of varied sizes — hydrodynamic diameter, polydispersity index, and size distribution. Results are cross-compared with UV-Vis surface-plasmon-resonance measurements of the same particles.
